# CS230-Portfolio
Computer Science program portfolio showcasing coursework and projects. 

**Portfolio Reflection
Briefly summarize The Gaming Room client and their software requirements.**

The Gaming Room is a client seeking to expand its existing Android game, Draw It or Lose It, into a distributed, web-based application capable of supporting multiple operating systems and devices. The application needed to maintain consistent gameplay whilst supporting multiple simultaneous game sessions, ensuring that game, team, and player names remained unique, and implementing recognised software design patterns to improve scalability and maintainability. The overall objective was to design a solution capable of supporting Windows, macOS, Linux, Android, and iOS users through a single web-based architecture.

**What did you do particularly well in developing this documentation?**

I certainly believe one of my greatest strengths was producing documentation that balanced technical accuracy with readability. Rather than simply describing individual technologies, I evaluated their advantages, limitations, and suitability for the client's specific requirements before making evidence-based recommendations. I also maintained consistency throughout the document by ensuring that architectural decisions, platform evaluations, security recommendations, and design patterns complemented one another instead of being presented as isolated concepts.

**What about the process of working through a design document did you find helpful when developing the code?**

Creating the design document before implementation provided a structured roadmap for development. By identifying architectural decisions, design patterns, and software requirements early in the process, I developed a clearer understanding of how individual components would interact before writing any code. This reduced uncertainty during implementation and made it easier to organise classes, APIs, and authentication features within the application.

**If you could choose one part of your work on these documents to revise, what would you pick? How would you improve it?**

If I were to revise one section, I would further expand the distributed systems architecture by incorporating modern cloud-native technologies such as container orchestration, automated scaling, continuous integration and deployment (CI/CD), and infrastructure as code. Whilst the current recommendations satisfy the client's requirements, these additions would better reflect contemporary enterprise software development practices and improve long-term scalability.

**How did you interpret the user's needs and implement them into your software design? Why is it so important to consider the user's needs when designing?**

I interpreted the client's requirements by identifying the application's functional objectives, long-term growth expectations, and platform compatibility needs before selecting appropriate architectural solutions. Design patterns such as Singleton and Iterator were chosen because they directly addressed requirements for maintaining unique game instances and efficiently managing collections of players and teams. Considering the user's needs is essential because successful software is ultimately measured by how proficiently it solves the client's problem rather than by the complexity of its implementation. A technically sophisticated application has little value if it fails to meet the expectations of those who will use it.
**
How did you approach designing software? What techniques or strategies would you use in the future to analyse and design a similar software application?**

Throughout this project, I approached software design by first analysing the client's business requirements before translating those requirements into technical solutions. I focused on selecting appropriate architectural patterns, evaluating operating platforms, considering scalability and security, and documenting the rationale behind each design decision. In future projects, I would continue using a requirements-driven approach whilst placing even greater emphasis on early risk analysis, cloud-native architecture, automated testing strategies, and security-by-design principles. I have learned that investing time in careful planning significantly simplifies implementation and produces software that is easier to maintain, extend, and scale over time.
